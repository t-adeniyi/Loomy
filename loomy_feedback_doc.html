Section 1

What's Working

✦ Working 01

Visual design & color palette

The pink palette is consistently praised as cohesive and well-suited for a creative platform. White space is widely appreciated — it feels professional, clean, and gives users breathing room. The overall design is described as polished and high-fidelity for a lo-fi stage. The undo/redo buttons and thoughtful button design were also noted.

"Strong color palette!!! wowowowo. Whitespace for creative freedom, undo/redo buttons, a lot of thought out buttons" — Ashley
"Choosing pink as the primary color actually goes so well with the overall vibe of the platform" — Lexie Zhang
"The aesthetics and color palette are really nice" — Brian
Visual designColor paletteWhite space
✦ Working 02

Human-centered & anti-AI stance

Multiple reviewers responded positively to the platform's explicit stance on keeping content human-made. The "avoid AI content" feature sparked the most discussion of any feature overall — and the majority view it as a meaningful differentiator.

"Love the 'avoid ai content' (to be able to identify) — keeping it human-centric" — Pushpita Hossain
"yayyy human-centered art platform that doesn't prefer AI" — Ashley
"Platform is trying to be maintained as human-centric work, I think a good message" — Brian

The term "Loom" resonates with reviewers — it's described as fun, memorable, and unique to the platform. The Loom workspace concept (connecting inspirations + work) is viewed as clever and well-matched to an artist audience.

Section 2

Priority Areas for Improvement

★ Critical C1

App purpose & value prop clarity

Several reviewers said they weren't sure what the core purpose of the app is. The distinction from Pinterest — which Loomy is inevitably compared to — needs to be made clearer upfront. Reviewers also want clearer guidance on what users are meant to do with their collections beyond just saving things.

"Not very clear what the purpose of the app is" — Ryan Lin
"It's heavily inspired by Pinterest, which is interesting, but what makes them stand out?" — Pushpita Hossain
"Right now it's not super clear what users will do with these collections — there should be recommendations for users in terms of what they wish to do with this specific piece of art" — Grace Li
Value propOnboardingPinterest comparisonFirst impression
★ Critical C2

Two flows feel too similar

The two user flows shown in the prototype don't feel distinct enough — they appear to solve the same problem rather than two clearly different ones. Prof. Hillman's suggested split: (1) a new user making their first Loom, and (2) a returning user revisiting saved inspiration and converting it into action.

"These two flows feel like one flow right now. I feel like we need to see two distinct problems being solved" — Prof. Hillman
User flowsIAProf. HillmanMust fix
★ Critical C3

IA hierarchy: Thread vs. Loom vs. Collection

The visual and conceptual distinction between a Thread (singular saved asset), a Loom (action workspace), and a Collection (organized group) must be clearer. This is a core IA problem — Prof. Hillman referenced the IA principle of objects as the framework for resolving it. Users need to immediately understand what each object is and how they relate to one another.

"The difference between adding a thread and making a loom needs to feel more clear" — Prof. Hillman
IAThreadLoomCollectionProf. Hillman
★ Critical C4

Navigation complexity & wayfinding

Too many visible buttons make it hard to find specific features (e.g. customizing a Loom). The navigation bar needs to show which section is currently active. Users need clearer direction on what to click next at each step.

"So many buttons to click, I would forget how to get to a single part of the app like customizing the Loom" — Ryan Lin + Lily Wu
"The navigation flow may need more directions so users know where to click as next step" — Lily Wu
"Consider your nav elements and how you show which section is active (collection, home, etc)" — Prof. Hillman
NavigationWayfindingActive stateCognitive load
High H1

Beginner vs. experienced user accessibility

Multiple reviewers independently noted the app feels designed for experienced or seasoned artists. Consider a beginner mode, onboarding scaffolding, or gamification elements to welcome users who are earlier in their creative journey.

"Slight essence that it might be designed for those with more experience — maybe have it be a little more beginner friendly?" — Miraya
"Sounds like it's made for seasoned artists — is there like a beginner format / gamification to encourage someone to do work?" — Brian
AccessibilityOnboardingBeginner modeGamification
High H2

AI content policy — needs clearer framing

The platform's stance on AI content is the most debated topic in the feedback. Reviewers want clarity on whether it's a full ban or a labeling/tagging system. A number strongly support tagging over banning to keep the platform open and inclusive. The policy should be documented clearly in UX copy.

"AI content — as far as out of scope — is to prevent it or encourage the creation of it? Clarified via question but would be good to add a couple more words there" — Prof. Hillman
"It shouldn't be a full ban, but rather a tag on AI content so people know what was made with AI" — David A.
AI policyUX copyLabelingContent moderation
High H3

Artist attribution & credits system

Multiple reviewers flagged that the platform needs a clear, visible system for crediting artists and linking to original creators. Transparency around attribution is flagged as essential — not just nice to have. Commenting-based accreditation was called out as a particularly interesting mechanic.

"I think it is also important to allow others who post art to be able to credit those that inspired them, and directly linking these credits to the creators — transparency is essential" — Grace Li
"I think artists' credit should be included in the website" — Freida Lin
"Commenting-based accreditation of the art is cool" — Brian
AttributionCreator creditIATransparency
Medium M1

Thread → Collection UX step feels disconnected

The two UI elements on the thread-to-collection step feel separate from each other due to being separately contained. Consider unifying their layout or flow so the relationship between them reads as a single cohesive step.

"Consider the UX on the thread to collection step. Those 2 UI elements feel separate due to them being separately contained" — Prof. Hillman
UXThread → CollectionVisual cohesion
Medium M2

Onboarding — option to skip steps

Allow users to skip the onboarding flow and jump directly to the main page. Not everyone wants to be walked through setup before exploring.

"For the sign-in and onboarding step, can the users choose to skip the steps and just go to the main page and start using?" — Lexie Zhang
OnboardingSkip optionUser autonomy
Medium M3

Button size & UX dimensions

Some buttons feel small, especially in a mobile context. Desktop layout may help but it's worth reviewing touch targets. Overall dimensions of certain UI elements were flagged as off.

"I do think some of the dimensions are off (UX wise for buttons and such)" — Pushpita Hossain
"Buttons are a bit small but on desktop is probably better" — Ashley
Button sizingMobile UXTouch targets
Medium M4

Desktop vs. app — justify the decision

The choice to design desktop-first needs to be better supported in user stories. Prof. Hillman noted the Loom page might be what sells the desktop experience — make that argument explicit. iPad was also raised as a potentially natural fit.

"It's fine to consider this as a desktop experience, but I also don't see why it couldn't be an app. Maybe the loom page sells me on why it's a better desktop experience" — Prof. Hillman
Platform decisionDesktopiPadUser stories
Medium M5

Prototype fidelity — intentional?

A few reviewers noted the prototype felt unusually high-fidelity for a lo-fi stage. Consider whether this is by design or whether the team wants to dial back fidelity to focus on flows.

"Very similar to Pinterest, very high fidelity for a lo-fi flow" — Ryan Lin

Add active nav state indicators so users always know where they are C4

High — address in next iteration

Address beginner accessibility — consider onboarding scaffolding or a "getting started" mode H1
